# Chapter 2: Latent Class Analysis: Testing for Hidden Groups

> *Clustering found two groups. But clustering is a data-carving exercise — it always finds groups, even in random noise. Latent Class Analysis asks a sharper question: is there statistical evidence for hidden groups, or is the legislature better described as a continuous spectrum?*

---

## From Carving to Modeling

Chapter 1 used five algorithms to divide legislators into groups. All five agreed: two groups, matching the two parties. But there's a subtle problem with that approach.

Clustering algorithms are **deterministic partitioning tools**. You tell them "find k groups" and they find k groups — always. If you hand k-means a dataset of random noise with no structure at all, it will still carve it into k tidy clusters. It can't tell you whether those clusters are *real* or *artifacts of the carving*.

**Latent Class Analysis** (LCA) takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of carving, it *models*. It proposes a statistical hypothesis: "There exists a hidden (latent) categorical variable with *K* values. Each value corresponds to a class of legislators who share a distinctive voting pattern. The observed votes are generated by each legislator's class membership."

The model can then be tested. Does adding a third class improve the fit enough to justify the extra complexity? Does the model with two classes actually fit better than the model with one (meaning no hidden groups at all)? These are the questions LCA answers.

## The Origin: Paul Lazarsfeld and the Hidden Variable

LCA was invented by **Paul Lazarsfeld** (1901–1976), an Austrian-American sociologist at Columbia University. In his 1950 contribution to *Studies in Social Psychology in World War II*, Lazarsfeld proposed that observable survey responses (agree/disagree on various statements) might be generated by an unobserved categorical trait — a person's "type." If you could discover the types, you could explain the pattern of responses.

Lazarsfeld called this framework **latent structure analysis**. But his original proposal was computationally intractable for the 1950s. It wasn't until **Leo Goodman** (1974) showed how to estimate LCA models using maximum likelihood that the method became practical. Goodman reformulated the problem as a log-linear model, bringing it into the mainstream statistical toolbox.

Today, LCA is standard in sociology, psychology, education, and public health — anywhere researchers suspect that a population contains distinct unobserved types generating observed behavior.

## How LCA Works: The Voting Profile Idea

Here's the intuition. Imagine the Kansas House has exactly two types of legislators:

- **Type A** votes Yea on tax cuts, Nay on social spending, Yea on gun bills
- **Type B** votes Nay on tax cuts, Yea on social spending, Nay on gun bills

If you knew each legislator's type, you could predict their entire voting record with high accuracy. LCA works backward: given the voting records, it infers the types.

### The Bernoulli Mixture Model

Formally, LCA models each vote as a **Bernoulli trial** (a coin flip with a class-specific probability). For each class *k* and each bill *j*, there's a parameter π_jk — the probability that a member of class *k* votes Yea on bill *j*.

```
P(vote_ij = Yea | legislator i belongs to class k) = π_jk
```

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**

Suppose there are K = 2 classes, and we're looking at one specific bill — say, a tax cut:

1. **Class 1** (let's call them "conservative") votes Yea on this bill 90% of the time: π = 0.90
2. **Class 2** ("liberal") votes Yea only 15% of the time: π = 0.15

A legislator who voted Yea on this bill is more likely to be in Class 1. But one vote isn't enough to be certain. LCA uses the pattern across *all* bills simultaneously: a legislator who votes Yea on tax cuts, Nay on social spending, and Yea on gun bills probably has a voting *profile* that matches Class 1.

### The Local Independence Assumption

LCA makes one critical assumption: **within each class, votes are independent.** Once you know which class a legislator belongs to, their vote on Bill A tells you nothing extra about their vote on Bill B.

This is the same kind of assumption IRT makes (Volume 4). And like IRT's assumption, it's not literally true — some bills are paired (a bill and its amendment), and topic-specific coalitions create local dependencies. But it's a useful simplification that makes estimation tractable, and violations tend to make the model add extra classes rather than fail entirely.

### Why Bernoulli Instead of Gaussian?

The clustering chapter used Gaussian Mixture Models, which assume the data follows a bell curve. But votes are binary — Yea or Nay — not continuous. The Bernoulli distribution is the correct likelihood for binary data. Using a Gaussian on binary data is like measuring the temperature of ice cream with a steak thermometer: it works approximately, but the tool is wrong for the job.

LCA's Bernoulli mixture uses **Full Information Maximum Likelihood** (FIML) to handle missing data (absences). Instead of dropping absent votes or imputing them, FIML simply computes the likelihood over the votes that *were* cast. A legislator who missed 30% of votes contributes less information per bill but doesn't require any guesswork about what they would have voted.

## Model Selection: How Many Classes?

LCA fits models with K = 1, 2, 3, ... up to K = 8 classes. How do you choose?

### BIC: The Bayesian Information Criterion

The **Bayesian Information Criterion** (BIC) balances fit against complexity. A model with more classes always fits the data better (more parameters = more flexibility), but at some point the extra classes are fitting noise rather than signal. BIC penalizes models with more parameters:

```
BIC = −2 · log-likelihood + p · log(n)
```

Where:
- **log-likelihood** measures how well the model fits the data (higher is better)
- **p** is the number of parameters (more classes = more parameters)
- **n** is the number of observations
- The penalty **p · log(n)** grows with both model complexity and sample size

**Plain English:** "How much better does this model fit, after subtracting a penalty for how complicated it is?"

Lower BIC is better. You plot BIC against K, and the minimum tells you the optimal number of classes.

### Entropy: How Clean Are the Assignments?

BIC tells you how many classes to use. **Entropy** tells you how confident the assignments are.

LCA gives each legislator a probability of belonging to each class. If a legislator has probabilities (0.99, 0.01), the assignment is clean — they're clearly in Class 1. If the probabilities are (0.52, 0.48), the assignment is fuzzy — they could go either way.

Entropy (in the LCA sense) is a summary of how clean the assignments are across all legislators:

```
Entropy = 1 − (observed uncertainty / maximum possible uncertainty)
```

- **Entropy = 1.0**: Every legislator is assigned to exactly one class with 100% certainty
- **Entropy > 0.80**: Good class separation — most legislators are clearly assigned
- **Entropy < 0.60**: Fuzzy boundaries — many legislators sit between classes

An entropy of 0.60 doesn't mean the model is wrong. It means the classes overlap — which is itself an important finding.

## The Salsa Effect: Mild, Medium, Hot

When BIC selects K > 2, there's a trap: the extra classes might not represent genuinely different voting *patterns*. They might just represent different *intensities* of the same pattern.

Think of it this way. A salsa manufacturer sells mild, medium, and hot salsa. All three contain the same ingredients (tomatoes, peppers, onions). They differ only in how much of each ingredient is used — more peppers means hotter. There's no qualitative difference between mild and hot; it's the same recipe at different settings.

The same thing can happen in LCA. A model with K = 3 might find "mild Republican," "medium Republican," and "Democrat" — three classes, but the two Republican classes have the same *pattern* of Yea/Nay across bills, just at different intensities. The same bills that one class votes Yea on, the other also votes Yea on — just at slightly different rates.

### Detecting the Salsa Effect

Tallgrass detects the salsa effect by computing the **Spearman rank correlation** between each pair of class profiles. A class profile is the vector of π values — the probability of Yea for each bill. If two profiles are highly correlated (Spearman r > 0.80), they're ordering bills the same way, just at different levels.

| Minimum pairwise r | Verdict |
|---------------------|---------|
| All pairs > 0.80 | **Salsa effect** — extra classes are quantitative, not qualitative |
| Mean > 0.80 but min < 0.80 | **Mixed** — mostly salsa, but at least one genuine difference |
| Mean < 0.80 | **Distinct** — genuinely different voting patterns |

In Kansas, the salsa effect is the typical finding when K > 2. The "extra" classes are simply more-or-less-partisan versions of the Republican profile. This aligns perfectly with Chapter 1's finding that within-party variation is continuous, not factional.

**Codebase:** `analysis/10_lca/lca.py` (`detect_salsa_effect()`, `SALSA_THRESHOLD = 0.80`)

## Cross-Validation with IRT

If LCA's classes are real, they should align with IRT's ideology scores. Tallgrass checks this by computing the mean IRT ideal point for each class and testing for **monotonicity**: when classes are sorted by their mean ideal point, are they in a consistent order?

If K = 3 classes have mean ideal points of −1.2, +0.3, and +0.9, they're monotonic — the classes are ordered along the same ideological spectrum that IRT estimated. This confirms that LCA and IRT are measuring the same underlying structure.

If the classes *aren't* monotonic — say Class 2 has a higher mean ideal point than Class 3 despite being in between on the bill profiles — that would suggest LCA has found a multi-dimensional structure that IRT's single dimension misses.

In Kansas, the classes are consistently monotonic, reinforcing the conclusion that the legislature is primarily one-dimensional (the party divide).

**Codebase:** `analysis/10_lca/lca.py` (`cross_validate_irt()`)

## The Impossibility Theorem

Here's an uncomfortable truth that LCA practitioners must confront. In 2006, psychologists **Gitta Lubke and Michael Neale** proved mathematically that **you cannot empirically distinguish between a categorical latent variable and a continuous one.**

What does this mean? If the true underlying structure is a continuous spectrum (like IRT assumes), a mixture model can always approximate it with enough classes. And if the true structure is categorical (like LCA assumes), a continuous model can always approximate it with enough flexibility. The data alone cannot tell you which is correct.

So when Tallgrass finds K = 2 classes that match the two parties, this does *not* prove that ideology is categorical. It's equally consistent with a continuous spectrum where the two parties happen to form two peaks. The K = 2 finding confirms that the dominant structure is a two-party split, but it says nothing about whether "Republican" and "Democrat" are discrete types or just convenient labels on a continuum.

This might seem like an unsatisfying conclusion, but it's an honest one. The value of LCA isn't in "proving" that groups exist — it's in providing a formal framework for testing how many groups the data supports and how cleanly they separate.

## Within-Party LCA

Just as Chapter 1 ran clustering within each party, Tallgrass runs LCA within the Republican and Democratic caucuses separately. The question: does the intra-party voting data support hidden subgroups?

The answer is usually K = 1 for both parties — no hidden subgroups within the party are supported by BIC. Occasionally K = 2 emerges for the Republicans, but when it does, the salsa effect test almost always detects quantitative grading rather than qualitative factions. The "moderate Republican" and "conservative Republican" classes have the same voting pattern at different intensities.

## LCA vs. Clustering: Why Both?

You might wonder why Tallgrass runs both clustering (Chapter 1) and LCA (this chapter) if they often reach the same conclusion. The reason is that they answer subtly different questions:

| Question | Clustering | LCA |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| "How many groups best describe the data?" | Silhouette score (heuristic) | BIC (formal model comparison) |
| "Is the group structure real or an artifact?" | Cross-method agreement (ARI) | Salsa effect test + entropy |
| "How certain is each assignment?" | Hard assignment only | Probability of membership per class |
| "Could this be a continuum instead of groups?" | Can't answer | Cross-validates with IRT monotonicity |

Clustering tells you *what the groups look like*. LCA tells you *whether you should believe in them*.

---

## Key Takeaway

Latent Class Analysis provides a probabilistic, model-based alternative to clustering. In Kansas, it confirms the clustering verdict: two classes matching the two parties, with no strong evidence for hidden subgroups. When more than two classes appear, the salsa effect test usually reveals that the extra classes are quantitative gradations (mild/medium/hot) rather than genuinely distinct voting patterns. The Lubke-Neale impossibility theorem reminds us that no amount of data can distinguish categorical from continuous latent structure — but the K = 2 finding robustly confirms that the two-party divide is the dominant organizing principle.
